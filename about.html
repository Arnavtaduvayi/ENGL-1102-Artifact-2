The exhibit

This digital exhibit looks at the 2010 film Never Let Me Go as a story about inequality, disposability, and how oppressive systems quietly teach people to accept being used. Hailsham feels gentle and safe on the surface, but the world of the film runs on a simple idea: some lives are worth less than others. Through memory, relationships, truth, and the question of the soul, this exhibit argues that the clones are fully human in every way that counts, but are still denied control over their own lives, dignity, and a future.

The exhibit focuses on what Kathy, Ruth, and Tommy feel and experience to show how inequality is kept in place not just through force, but through education, "careful language," and emotional control. By "careful language" we mean the soft words the school uses to cover up what is really happening. The clones are called "students." The staff are "guardians." Surgery that removes their organs is a "donation." Death is called "completion." The people involved are "carers" and "donors." None of these words are lies, but each one is a gentle label for something brutal. This is the same trick the "Designed Inequality" corridor takes apart: the guardians' kindness is part of the system, not the opposite of it. Nathan Snaza calls this "the failure of humanizing education," where the polite, caring lessons at Hailsham are exactly what prepare the students to accept being used.

Purpose & audience

The purpose of this website is to use the film's fictional world to highlight real systems that treat certain people as more disposable than others. This exhibit is for viewers interested in dystopian stories, ethics, and social inequality today. It asks them to think about how societies justify shutting people out while still looking caring and polite.

More than just analyzing the film, this project argues that Never Let Me Go matters because it shows how easily personhood can be taken away when a person's value is tied to their usefulness. This view is shared by scholars who read the story as social critique. Wen Guo reads the clones as "the other": a group pushed outside the category of the fully human so that their pain does not count as pain. Mimi Wong reads them as a kind of racial metaphor and links their fate to real-world organ trafficking, commercial surrogacy, and migrant labor. The exhibit follows this thread, especially in the "Who Counts as Human?" and "The World We Made" corridors, and invites its audience to ask who, in our own world, is still being treated as disposable.

How the exhibit works

The site is built so that visitors do not just read about Hailsham. They walk through it. Every design choice is meant to make the viewer feel the same slow discovery the clones go through in the film.

  • Move your cursor to uncover hidden lines. Short sentences in red are hidden on every page. They only appear when your cursor passes over them. These are the things Hailsham does not say out loud, such as "they called it a school", "it was a farm", and "love doesn't save you". The viewer has to actively search for the truth, the same way Kathy and Tommy have to piece together what Hailsham really is.
  • The dark theme stands for what is hidden. The black and brown colours are not just for mood. They show a world where most of the truth is kept in shadow. The small warm glow around the cursor acts like a candle. You can only see a small circle of the room at a time, so you never get the full picture at once. This matches how the clones only learn small pieces of their fate, slowly, across many years.
  • Five doors, five corridors. On the home page there are five wooden doors. The first four hold the four main arguments of the exhibit inside the story: memory and identity, relationships and control, truth and deception, and who counts as human. The fifth door steps outside the story and points at the real world. Doors are used because Hailsham is full of rules about what students may enter, leave, or know. Opening one here means choosing to look at something the Guardians would rather keep closed.
  • We want you to feel like a student at Hailsham. The goal is not a normal essay website. The goal is an experience. You arrive at a quiet, pretty place. You are told very little. You have to move the cursor, open doors, and read between the lines to learn what is really going on. By the time you reach the last room, the exhibit wants you to feel the same discomfort the clones feel: that the calm surface was hiding something cruel the whole time.

Walk through the corridors

The five doors on the home page open into five different parts of the argument the two sections above describe. The first four stay inside the film. The fifth one steps out of it and into our world.

  • Manufactured Humanity. You enter the grounds of Hailsham as Kathy remembers them: wide fields, dorm windows in winter, tokens traded for drawings. The writing here is a paraphrase in Kathy's voice, not a quote from the film's script. This corridor supports the claim in "The exhibit" that oppressive systems work best when they look kind. The warmth you feel in this room is the same warmth the students were given so they would not push back.
  • Love Without Agency. You see Kathy, Tommy, and Ruth caught in a love triangle that has no honest way out. This corridor puts a human face on the "emotional control" mentioned in "The exhibit". The three friends feel everything a person feels, but they do not get to choose where their feelings lead.
  • Designed Inequality. You see the truth the guardians keep back. The students were made to donate their organs, and the classes, the paintings, and the careful schedule were the wrapping on top of that. This is where the word "disposable" from "Purpose & audience" becomes concrete.
  • Who Counts as Human? You stand with Kathy as she tries to prove her humanity through songs, art, and the love she feels. This corridor asks the audience the same question the main argument rests on: what makes a person a person, and who gets to decide?
  • The World We Made. You step outside the film. This corridor names real systems that work the way Hailsham does: kidney sales in Egypt, Pakistan, and India, the Gulf kafala system that traps migrant workers, US prison labor, and boarding schools set up for Indigenous children in Canada and the United States. It turns the closing question from "Purpose & audience" (who, in our own world, is still being treated as disposable) from a feeling about a film into a fact about our daily lives.
