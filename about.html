The exhibit

This digital exhibit looks at Never Let Me Go as a story about inequality, disposability, and how oppressive systems quietly teach people to accept being used. Hailsham feels gentle and safe on the surface, but the world of the novel runs on a simple idea: some lives are worth less than others. Through memory, relationships, truth, and the question of the soul, this exhibit argues that the clones are fully human in every way that counts, but are still denied control over their own lives, dignity, and a future. By focusing on what Kathy, Ruth, and Tommy feel and experience, the exhibit shows how inequality is kept in place not just through force, but through education, careful language, and emotional control.

Purpose & audience

The purpose of this website is to use Ishiguro's fictional world to highlight real systems that treat certain people as more disposable than others. This exhibit is for readers interested in dystopian fiction, ethics, and social inequality today. It asks viewers to think about how societies justify shutting people out while still looking caring and polite. More than just analyzing the novel, this project argues that Never Let Me Go matters because it shows how easily personhood can be taken away when a person's value is tied to their usefulness. The exhibit invites its audience to ask who, in our own world, is still being treated as disposable.

How the exhibit works

The site is built so that visitors do not just read about Hailsham. They walk through it. Every design choice is meant to make the reader feel the same slow discovery the clones go through in the novel.

  • Move your cursor to uncover hidden lines. Short sentences in red are hidden on every page. They only appear when your cursor passes over them. These are the things Hailsham does not say out loud, such as "they called it a school", "it was a farm", and "love doesn't save you". The student has to actively search for the truth, the same way Kathy and Tommy have to piece together what Hailsham really is.
  • The dark theme stands for what is hidden. The black and brown colours are not just for mood. They show a world where most of the truth is kept in shadow. The small warm glow around the cursor acts like a candle. You can only see a small circle of the room at a time, so you never get the full picture at once. This matches how the clones only learn small pieces of their fate, slowly, across many years.
  • The four doors are the four themes. On the home page there are four wooden doors. Each door leads to one main argument of the exhibit: memory and identity, relationships and control, truth and deception, and who counts as human. Doors are used because Hailsham is full of rules about what students may enter, leave, or know. Opening a door here means choosing to look at something the Guardians would rather keep closed.
  • We want you to feel like a student at Hailsham. The goal is not a normal essay website. The goal is an experience. You arrive at a quiet, pretty place. You are told very little. You have to move the cursor, open doors, and read between the lines to learn what is really going on. By the time you reach the last room, the exhibit wants you to feel the same discomfort the clones feel: that the calm surface was hiding something cruel the whole time.
