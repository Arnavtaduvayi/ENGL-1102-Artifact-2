Hailsham: Manufactured Humanity

Hailsham was supposed to feel like home. And in many ways, it did — the wide open grounds, the changing seasons, the rhythm of classes and meals and evenings spent doing nothing in particular. We didn't question it then. Children rarely do. We collected tokens, traded drawings, whispered about the Gallery as though it held some great secret about who we were. Maybe it did.

What stays with you isn't the big moments. It's the small ones. The smell of the grass after rain. The way the light fell through the dormitory windows in winter. A look exchanged across a room that meant everything and was never spoken about again. Kathy, Tommy, Ruth — the three of us tangled up in something we didn't yet have the words for.

We thought we had time. That's the cruelest part of it. Hailsham felt endless when we were inside it, and now it feels like it lasted no longer than a held breath.
